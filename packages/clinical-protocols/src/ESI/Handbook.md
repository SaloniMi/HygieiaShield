# ESI v5 Triage Reference (Emergency Severity Index, 5th Edition)

## What is ESI
A 5-level ED triage tool. Assigns acuity level 1 (most urgent) → 5 (least urgent) by evaluating physiologic stability and predicted resource needs. Used by 94% of US EDs. Does NOT define time-to-physician intervals.

---

## Algorithm: 4 Decision Points in Order

```
A → B → C → D
```

Higher-acuity patients resolve at A or B. Only stable patients reach C/D.

---

## Decision Point A — ESI Level 1
**Question:** Does this patient require an immediate lifesaving intervention?

**Assign ESI 1 if ANY present:**
- Unresponsive (nonverbal + not following commands, OR requires noxious stimulus — P/U on AVPU scale)
- Pulseless / apneic / cardiac or pulmonary arrest (or imminent)
- Severe respiratory distress requiring intubation or emergent ventilation
- Profound hypotension with signs of hypoperfusion
- Anaphylaxis (unstable)
- Severe bradycardia or tachycardia with instability
- Hypoglycemia with altered consciousness
- Flaccid infant
- Penetrating trauma to head/neck/chest/abdomen requiring intervention
- SpO2 <90% (not patient's baseline) with other signs of respiratory compromise

**Lifesaving interventions include:**
- Airway: assisted ventilation, intubation, surgical airway, emergent NIPPV
- Electrical: defibrillation, emergent cardioversion, external pacing
- Procedures: chest needle decompression, pericardiocentesis, open thoracotomy
- Hemodynamics: significant IV fluid resuscitation, blood products, hemorrhage control
- Medications: adenosine, atropine, dextrose, dopamine, epinephrine (incl. IM anaphylaxis), naloxone

> Diagnostics (CT for stroke, cath lab for stable MI) are NOT lifesaving interventions.
> Discharge likelihood does NOT affect ESI 1 assignment (e.g. hypoglycemia may go home).

## Decision Point B — ESI Level 2
**Question:** Is this a high-risk presentation? New confusion/lethargy/disorientation? Severe pain/distress?

**Assign ESI 2 if ANY present:**

### High-risk situations (potential threat to life, limb, sight, or organ):
- Active chest pain suspicious for ACS (but not yet requiring lifesaving intervention)
- Stroke signs not meeting level-1 criteria
- Possible ectopic pregnancy, hemodynamically stable
- Immunocompromised (chemo, transplant) with fever
- Transplant recipient with any infection or rejection sign
- Needle-stick in healthcare worker (time-sensitive PEP)
- Sexual assault survivor
- Postpartum hemorrhage
- Increasing respiratory effort (not yet level 1)
- Actively suicidal or homicidal
- Pregnant/postpartum with SBP <90 or >150 (even without other symptoms)
- Pregnant with chest pain, shortness of breath, severe headache, or abdominal pain
- Heavy vaginal bleeding (soaking pad/hour, plum-sized clots, bright red not slowing)
- Testicular or ovarian torsion (time-sensitive)
- Severe flank pain (possible urosepsis or renal colic)
- Urosepsis: UTI + elderly + back pain/chills/rigors
- Toxic ingestion with AMS, abnormal HR/rhythm, or respiratory changes
- Thunderclap headache or headache + neck stiffness/fever/AMS/stroke signs
- Patient post-ictal (assign ESI 2 for altered mental status)
- Epistaxis with posterior bleed or anticoagulation/thrombocytopenia
- Airway emergency: unable to manage secretions, audible stridor
- Falls ≥20 ft, ejection from vehicle, mechanical extrication
- Penetrating trauma head/neck/chest/abdomen without hemodynamic instability
- Orthopedic injury with neurovascular compromise, compartment syndrome, or amputation
- Mental/behavioral: suicidal, homicidal, psychotic, violent

### Altered Mental Status (new onset):
- Confusion, lethargy, disorientation, agitation, somnolence, belligerence = ESI 2
- If history unknown and patient appears confused → assume new onset → ESI 2
- AMS causes: hypoxia, hypoglycemia, hyponatremia, underperfusion, ↑ICP, toxicology

### Severe pain or distress:
- Pain ≥7/10 from systemic cause (renal colic, sickle cell, cancer) → ESI 2
- Pain from orthopedic injury without neurovascular compromise → may wait, assign by resources
- Severe psychological distress: sexual assault, suicidal ideation/plan/attempt, domestic violence, acute grief, behavioral outbursts, combativeness, prenatal loss → ESI 2

> **Geriatric note:** Elderly patients may not mount immune/compensatory responses due to age or beta-blockers, corticosteroids. Interpret vitals in context of medications. Frequently undertriaged — be vigilant.
> **Pediatric note:** Subtle AMS in a child may signal hemodynamic compromise — treat as high risk.

## Decision Point C — ESI Levels 3 / 4 / 5
**Question:** How many different types of resources will this patient need?

Only reached if ESI 1 and 2 have been ruled out.

| Resources needed | ESI Level |
|---|---|
| 0 | 5 |
| 1 | 4 |
| 2 or more | 3 |

### Count TYPES, not individual tests:
- All blood labs together = 1 resource
- All urine labs together = 1 resource (same bucket as blood if ordered together)
- CBC + electrolytes + coags = **1 resource** (all labs)
- CBC + chest X-ray = **2 resources** (labs + imaging)
- Chest X-ray + abdominal X-ray = **1 resource** (both imaging)
- C-spine film + head CT = **2 resources** (X-ray + CT)

### ESI Resources ✓
- Labs: blood, urine
- ECG, radiographs (X-ray)
- CT, MRI, ultrasound, angiography
- IV fluids (hydration — not saline lock)
- IV, IM, or nebulized medications
- Specialty consultation
- Simple procedure = 1 (laceration repair, urinary catheter)
- Complex procedure = 2 (procedural sedation)

### NOT ESI Resources ✗
- History & physical exam (including pelvic)
- Point-of-care testing
- Saline or heparin lock
- Oral medications
- Tetanus immunization
- Prescription refills
- Phone call to PCP
- Simple wound care (dressings, recheck)
- Crutches, splints, slings

### Example assignments:
| Patient | Resources | ESI |
|---|---|---|
| 3-year-old ear pain, immunized, vitals WNL | Exam + prescription only | 5 |
| Lost rescue inhaler, asymptomatic | Exam + prescription only | 5 |
| 19-year-old sore throat | Culture (labs) | 4 |
| 29-year-old dysuria, female | Urine + culture + urine pregnancy (all labs = 1) | 4 |
| 22-year-old RLQ abdominal pain | Labs + IV fluids + CT + surgical consult | 3 |
| 45-year-old leg swelling after 12-hr car trip | Labs + vascular ultrasound | 3 |

## Decision Point D — Reassess for High-Risk Vital Signs
**Question:** Are vital signs outside normal parameters?

Applies to patients initially assigned ESI 3/4/5. Obtain a full set of vitals. If abnormal → uptriage.

### Adult high-risk thresholds (>18 years):
| Measure | High-risk threshold |
|---|---|
| Heart rate | >100 bpm |
| Respiratory rate | >20 breaths/min |
| SpO2 | <92% |

### Pediatric high-risk thresholds:
| Age | HR | RR |
|---|---|---|
| <1 month | >190 | >60 |
| 1–12 months | >180 | >55 |
| 1–3 years | >140 | >40 |
| 3–5 years | >120 | >35 |
| 5–12 years | >120 | >30 |
| 12–18 years | >100 | >20 |

SpO2 <92% is high-risk at all ages.

### Normal pediatric vital sign ranges (for reference):
| Age | HR (bpm) | RR (breaths/min) | Systolic BP (mmHg) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Neonate <1 mo | 90–190 | 35–60 | 67–84 |
| Infant 1–12 mo | 90–180 | 30–55 | 72–104 |
| Toddler 1–3 yr | 80–140 | 22–40 | 86–104 |
| Preschool 3–5 yr | 65–120 | 18–35 | 89–112 |
| School age 5–12 yr | 70–120 | 16–30 | 90–115 |
| Adolescent 12–18 yr | 60–100 | 12–20 | 100–130 |

### Pediatric fever red flags:
| Age | Red flag temperature |
|---|---|
| <28 days | >38°C (100.4°F) → assign at least ESI 2 |
| 1–3 months | >38°C (100.4°F) → consider ESI 2 |
| >3 months | >39°C (102.2°F) or <36°C (96.8°F) + incomplete immunizations or no obvious fever source → consider ESI 2 or 3 |

> Vital signs must be interpreted in context of medications (beta-blockers blunt tachycardia, corticosteroids blunt immune response). "Normal" vitals on medications may still indicate serious illness.

## Bias & Undertriage Warning
Clinical evidence shows systematic undertriage in:
- Black, Hispanic, and Indigenous patients
- Female patients (especially cardiac presentations)
- Geriatric patients (>65, especially >80)
- Patients with psychiatric/behavioral history or substance use
- Patients labeled "difficult"

**Do not adjust ESI level based on ED crowding, bed availability, or staff capacity.** ESI reflects patient acuity, not ED state. ESI level should never be used to manipulate door-to-ECG or door-to-CT metrics.

---

## Key Rules Summary
1. Follow A → B → C → D in order. Stop at first match.
2. ESI 1 and 2 are based on acuity only. Resources are irrelevant.
3. ESI 3/4/5 are based on resource count. Acuity has already been ruled out.
4. Count resource *types*, not individual orders.
5. H&P, oral meds, POC testing, splints, and prescriptions are NEVER resources.
6. Obtain full vitals for all ESI 3/4/5 patients — may trigger uptriage to ESI 2.
7. ESI level should not change after provider evaluation unless condition changes before placement.
8. Do not assign ESI without assessment. Do not assign based on environmental conditions.
